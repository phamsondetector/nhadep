Lu Ban - God of Construction and Patron Saint of Carpentry


Lu Ban is a legendary figure closely associated with the construction industry and carpentry profession in East Asia, particularly in Vietnam and China. He was not only a highly skilled craftsman but is also revered as the founding master of carpentry, architecture, and traditional construction. 1. Who is Lu Ban?


Lu Ban, whose real name was Gong Du Ban (also known as Gong Thau Ban or Gong Du Zi), was born around 507 BC in the State of Lu (now in Shandong Province, China), and lived at the end of the Spring and Autumn period to the beginning of the Warring States period. He belonged to the artisan clan of the State of Lu and resided in the Qufu region. 
From a young age, Lu Ban was renowned as a talented carpenter, metalworker, and engineer, with deep knowledge of wood, stone, and metal. According to some legends, he once secluded himself for cultivation on Mount Lịch Sơn (or Mount Tai) for about 13 years, realizing the “heavenly mandate” to spread craftsmanship for the benefit of the people. He not only invented tools but also designed weapons and military structures; however, folklore primarily remembers him in his role as the “ancestor of carpentry”. 
In Vietnam and many East Asian countries, Lu Ban is regarded as a master of ancient architecture who laid the foundation for traditional construction knowledge. His memorial day (the 13th day of the 6th lunar month) is still honored by many carpenters and builders to pray for safety and talent.

2. Famous legends about Lu Ban

Lu Ban’s life is intertwined with countless miraculous legends that demonstrate his talent as the “xảo thánh” (saint of dexterity), comparable to Confucius – the “supreme saint and first teacher”. Here are some of the most prominent stories:
• Invention of the saw and carpentry tools: According to folklore, Lu Ban observed the serrated edges of reed leaves (or bamboo) that cut into the hand and invented the saw blade. He also created the plane, chisel, carpenter’s square, ink line (for drawing straight lines), compass, and more. These tools remain the foundation of carpentry to this day. 
• Wooden bird (mộc diên/mộc thước) that could fly for three days: This is the most famous legend from the Mozi. Lu Ban assembled wood into a bird (or kite) that could fly around the city of Song for three days without falling. Some legends say he even created a wooden eagle capable of carrying people through the sky. 
• Cloud ladder (vân thê) and iron hook (câu cường/câu cự): Lu Ban invented the cloud ladder for the Chu army to attack the city of Song and iron hooks to pull enemy boats on the river. He once “matched wits” with Mozi (who advocated against war) and conceded, proving both his talent and his respect for moral principles. 
• Divine appearance to help build the Holy Mother Temple: During the Song Dynasty, when builders could not solve the pillar issue for the Holy Mother Temple (Thai Nguyen), an old man appeared and used wood scraps to create a model of a palace without pillars, transferring the load to the roof and corridors. The old man then disappeared; folklore believes it was Lu Ban manifesting. A shrine was built in his honor nearby. 
• Birth and ascension to immortality: Legend says that when Lu Ban was born, a fragrant aroma filled the room and cranes flocked to gather. When he passed away, he became an immortal. These stories emphasize that he is the “Xảo Thánh Tiên Sư” – the saint of dexterity blessed by the gods. 
These legends are both historical (based on the Mozi and Warring States texts) and mythical, helping Lu Ban become a symbol of dexterity and creativity.

3. Lu Ban’s influence on the construction industry

Lu Ban’s influence on the construction industry is profound and has endured for thousands of years, especially in East Asian culture:
• Ancestor of carpentry and construction: Carpenters, masons, and traditional architects in Vietnam, China, Japan, and beyond all worship Lu Ban as their patron saint. He is considered the “father” of folk carpentry and construction. Many ancient projects include rituals to worship Lu Ban before groundbreaking. 
• Lu Ban Ruler – the immortal feng shui tool: He is credited with creating the Lu Ban Ruler (two main types: 52.2 cm for openings such as doors and windows; 42.9 cm for structural elements such as stoves, bases, and steps). This ruler combines standard measurement with feng shui sections (Great Luck, Heavenly Talent, Poetry Loss, etc.) to select dimensions that bring luck, wealth, and health to the homeowner. To this day, Vietnamese builders still use the Lu Ban Ruler when constructing houses, making interiors, and especially in traditional feng shui practices. 
• Lu Ban Jing – the construction classic: He left behind (or it was recorded under his name) the book Lu Ban Jing (魯班經) – a classic document on house-building techniques, wooden furniture making, architectural feng shui, selecting auspicious days, talismans to ward off evil, and house protection. This book was the “bedside companion” of ancient carpenters, blending practical techniques with yin-yang and five elements philosophy. In Vietnam, Lu Ban Jing is still printed and consulted when building houses. 
• Lu Ban talismans and spiritual culture: Talismans and spells related to construction (warding off evil, protecting structures) all bear the name “Lu Ban talisman”. This originates from the knowledge he passed down so that carpenters could protect themselves from oppression or misfortune. 
In summary, Lu Ban was not only the creator of tools but also shaped the entire body of traditional East Asian construction knowledge: from practical techniques to feng shui and spirituality. Even in the modern era, his name lives on through the Lu Ban Ruler, Lu Ban Jing, and the worship rituals of craftsmen.
